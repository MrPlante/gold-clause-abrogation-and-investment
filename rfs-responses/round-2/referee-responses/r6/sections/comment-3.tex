\bigskip

\noindent We acknowledge this observation. The confidence intervals around individual year estimates are wide given two-way clustered standard errors in a sample of this size, and individual year-to-year pairwise comparisons will often fail to reach significance.

\medskip
\noindent The correct test for parallel trends is not whether each pre-treatment year can be individually distinguished from 1933 or 1934, but whether the pre-treatment coefficients are jointly zero relative to the omitted category (1932) and whether they exhibit a systematic trend leading into the treatment period. The pre-treatment coefficients in column 2 of Table 1 are $+0.026$, $+0.012$, $+0.022$, $-0.032$, and $-0.007$ for 1927 through 1931, respectively, none of which is statistically significant. Crucially, the sequence shows no monotone drift toward negative values: it oscillates around zero with no directional trend, which is the relevant pattern for the parallel trends assumption.

\medskip
\noindent The 1930 dip ($-0.032$, insignificant) deserves a brief comment. In 1930 the Great Depression was already deepening---bank failures were accelerating and credit conditions were tightening sharply. Since gold-exposed firms were systematically more leveraged (Table 2), they may have been somewhat more sensitive to this general credit contraction than unexposed firms, independent of gold clause exposure. The fact that this coefficient is small and insignificant, and is followed by coefficients of $-0.007$ and $0.000$ (omitted) in 1931--1932, is more consistent with occasional noise than with a persistent pre-trend.

\medskip
\noindent That said, we acknowledge that limited statistical power prevents us from rejecting the null that the 1930 coefficient equals the 1933 or 1934 coefficient. We have added a sentence to Section 5 noting that the parallel trends argument rests on the full pre-treatment pattern rather than year-by-year pairwise tests, and that the absence of a visible trend in the point estimates provides the primary support for this assumption.
